# Voice Rules — English

## Tone & Formality

The writing operates in a formal academic register governed by exclusive declarative mood — no imperatives or interrogatives appear. Contractions are entirely absent; all verb forms appear as fully spelled-out constructions. Passive voice occurs with moderate frequency, used instrumentally to foreground processes and objects of study rather than agents. No sentence begins with a coordinating conjunction; all sentences open with either a fronted adverbial phrase, a subject pronoun, or a determiner. Hedging language is minimal — claims are asserted directly without epistemic qualifiers such as modals of possibility or evidential markers. The consistent use of first-person plural pronoun establishes an authorial presence while maintaining institutional distance from the reader. The overall effect is one of measured authority: personal enough to claim ownership of the work, formal enough to satisfy disciplinary convention.

## Word Choice

Lexical selection is heavily dominated by Latinate vocabulary, with abstract nominalizations forming the structural backbone of the prose. The noun-to-verb ratio is high; finite verbs tend to be semantically light or copular, carrying less lexical weight than their accompanying noun phrases. Modifier density is moderate, with attributive adjectives occasionally appearing in pairs before key nouns. A recurring syntactic frame structures much of the prose: a fronted prepositional phrase, followed by the first-person plural pronoun, followed by a present-tense verb and its complement (direct object or infinitive clause). Compound nouns are formed productively from smaller nominal units joined attributively. Abstract nouns referring to processes, methods, and properties significantly outnumber concrete nouns. The lexicon favors evaluative descriptors that position the writer's contributions relative to existing work.

## Sentence Structure

Sentences are medium to long, with complexity achieved primarily through subordination rather than coordination. Relative clauses introduced by relative pronouns embed additional information to the right of the main clause, producing a consistently right-branching architecture. Fronted adverbial phrases — particularly temporal and thematic markers — open the majority of sentences, creating a predictable, almost formulaic rhythm. The style is cumulative rather than periodic: the main clause appears early in the sentence, with elaborating material appended afterward in a series of prepositional and relative expansions. A small set of recurring introductory frames — chiefly a prepositional phrase of location or scope followed by the subject-verb core — gives the prose a structured cadence. Independent clauses are joined primarily through subordinating conjunctions and conjunctive adverbs such as "as well as" rather than through coordination with "and".

None present in sample.

## Abbreviations & Contractions

Contractions are entirely absent from the sample; all negations and auxiliary-verb combinations are written in full form (e.g., "cannot" rather than "can't", "is evaluated" rather than "is being evaluated"). Acronyms are introduced through a consistent pattern: the full expanded form appears first in the text, immediately followed by the abbreviated form in parentheses to establish the referent. Subsequent mentions use the abbreviation alone. All acronyms are capitalized in standard uppercase style.

## Tense & Pronouns

The dominant tense is present simple, used for stating findings, describing methods, and making claims of general validity. Tense shifts are minimal — the present tense is maintained consistently throughout. The first-person plural pronoun "we" serves as the primary subject across nearly all clauses, creating a collective authorial voice. The possessive determiner "our" parallels this pattern. Subjects are always explicit; no elliptical or implicit subject constructions occur. Person and number remain consistent: no shift to first-person singular, second-person, or third-person impersonal constructions.

## Markdown Conventions

Headings use up to three levels with standard capitalization. Unordered lists employ the dash marker. Bold and italic emphasis are both used for highlighting. Code blocks use backtick-fenced style with language identifiers. Standard blank-line spacing separates sections, headings, and list items.
